\section{Theory}
\subsection{Ferrofluids}

Ferrofluid is a liquid that becomes strongly magnetized in the presence of a magnetic field. It is made up of tiny magnetic particles (typically iron oxide) suspended in a carrier fluid. These particles are in nanometer scale so that they do not settle due to gravity, remaining evenly dispersed throughout the liquid thanks to surfactants that prevent clumping.

When exposed to a magnetic field, ferrofluids form distinctive spiky patterns along the magnetic field lines fig(\ref{ferrofluid}). This unique property makes them useful in various applications, including sealing rotating shafts in hard drives, cooling loudspeakers, and as a medium for magnetic field visualization in research. They are also studied for potential use in medical applications, such as targeted drug delivery. 

\subsubsection{Physical Properties}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Superparamagnetism
    
    Under the magnetic field, MNPs align with the field direction. Once the field be removed, the induced magnetic field will decrease to 0. 

    \item Thermal Stability
    
    Ferrofluids are thermally stable and can withstand a wide range of temperatures without significant degradation. However, extreme temperatures can cause particle agglomeration, reducing their effectiveness.

    \item Viscosity
    
    The viscosity of a ferrofluid changes with the strength of an applied magnetic field. In the presence of a strong field, it can behave more like a solid, increasing its resistance to flow. Without a magnetic field, it behaves like a normal liquid.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Magnetic Relaxation}
\subsubsection{Introduction}
Magnetic relaxation refers to the processes by which the magnetization of a material returns to its equilibrium state after being disturbed.  In the case of $\text{Fe}_3 \text{O}_4$ fluid, the behavior of the nanoparticles under different magnetic field intensity involves complicated phenomenon behind. In short, it may be described in two mechanisms, Brownian relaxation and Néel relaxation. When we applied a magnetic field to $\text{Fe}_3 \text{O}_4$ fluid, in order to return to the equilibrium state, it would go through a two-step response, Néel relaxation occurred first and then Brownian relaxation. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item Néel Relaxation
    
    This regime is especially important in superparamagnetism, which appears in ferrimagnetic nanoparticles. When these particles are small enough, they behave as a single magnetic domain, and their overall magnetization can rapidly change direction. The magnetization of Néel regime is driven by physical rotation(flipping) of the particle itself. Because of the MNPs' anisotropy, there usually exists two stable orientations with $180^{\circ}$ differences in the MNPs and the mean time between two flips is called Néel relaxation time $\tau_{N}$. 
    \begin{equation}
         \tau_{N}=\tau_{0}e^{\frac{K_{u}V_{M}}{K_{B}T}}
    \end{equation}
    
    \item Brownian Relaxation
    
    The Brownian regime refers to the state in which the magnetization of a MnPs changes due to the physical rotation of the entire particle, rather than internal flipping of the magnetic moment as in the Néel regime. In this case, the change in magnetization occurs because the particle itself undergoes Brownian motion, which is the random movement of particles in a fluid due to thermal fluctuations.
    In the Brownian regime, the magnetization direction of the particle stays aligned with the particle's body, and the entire NP must rotate to reorient its magnetization. This rotation is influenced by factors like the viscosity of the surrounding medium and the particle's size. The characteristic time for these reorientations is called the Brownian relaxation time.

    \begin{equation}
        \tau_{B}=\frac{3\eta V_{H}}{K_{B}T}
        \label{tauB}
    \end{equation}
    
    Both mechanisms occur at the same time when the magnetic field changes, but the faster one (the one with a smaller time constant) dominates the relaxation process (Eq.\ref{tau eff}).

    \begin{equation}
        \frac{1}{\tau}=\frac{1}{\tau_{N}}+\frac{1}{\tau_{B}}
        \label{tau eff}
    \end{equation}

    where $\tau_{0}$, $\tau$, $K_{u}$, $V_{M}$, $V_{H}$, $K_{B}$, $\eta$, $T$ denoted attempt time, effective relaxation time, anisotropy constant, primary volume of the MNPs(magnetic nano particles), hydrodynamic volume of the MNPs, Boltzmann constant, viscosity of the solvent, temperature, respectively. 
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Complex Susceptibility}

\subsubsection{Derivation}
When the magnetic particles are subjected to an alternating magnetic field with angular frequency $\omega$, its instantaneous magnetization can be written in Eq.\ref{eq:magnetization}, 

\begin{equation}
    \rm{M(t)}=\chi H_0 e^{i \omega t}
    % \approx \frac{\mu_0 M}{B}
    \label{eq:magnetization}
\end{equation}

\noindent
and the change of the magnetization is characterized by Eq.\ref{eq:change of magnetization} with $\rm{M_0}=\chi_0 \rm{H_0} e^{i \omega t}$, $\chi_0$ being the DC susceptibility.

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\rm{M(t)}}{dt}=-\frac{\rm{M(t)}-\rm{M_0}}{\tau}
    \label{eq:change of magnetization}
\end{equation}

% \noindent
Plugging Eq.\ref{eq:magnetization} into Eq.\ref{eq:change of magnetization} will yield Eq.\ref{eq:complex magnetization}.

\begin{equation}
    \chi=\chi_0 \frac{1}{1+i \omega \tau}
    \label{eq:complex magnetization}
\end{equation}

\noindent
Then, we rearrange Eq.\ref{eq:complex magnetization} to be Eq.\ref{eq:complex magnetization beauti}. The real and complex parts are commonly denoted by $\chi'$ and $\chi''$, respectively. Alternatively, we can use a phase ($\theta$) to describe the lag with respect to the external field.

\begin{equation}
    \chi=\chi_0 \frac{1}{1+ (\omega \tau)^2}-i \chi_0 \frac{\omega \tau}{1+(\omega \tau)^2} \\
    = \chi'-i \chi''=|\chi| e^{i \theta}
    \label{eq:complex magnetization beauti}
\end{equation}

\noindent
Moreover, the oscillation of the external field can be incorporated  into susceptibility (Eq.\ref{eq:phaser susceptibility derivation}), 

\begin{equation}
    \rm{M(t)}=(\chi e^{i \omega t}) \rm{H}_0 = \boldmath{\chi} \rm{H}_0
    \label{eq:phaser susceptibility derivation}
\end{equation}

\noindent
and denote by $\bm{\chi}$ (Eq.\ref{eq:phaser susceptibiity}).

\begin{equation}
    \bm{\chi}=(\chi) e^{i \omega t}=(|\chi|e^{i \theta}) e^{i \omega t} =|\chi|e^{i( \omega t+\theta)}
    \label{eq:phaser susceptibiity}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Peak of Imaginary Part}
In order to find the frequency that corresponds to the highest imaginary part of the susceptibility ($\chi''$), we differentiate $\chi''$ with respect to $\omega$ and set it to be 0 (Eq.\ref{eq:diff imaginary part}).

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d \chi''}{d \omega}= \chi_0 \frac{\tau- \omega^2 \tau^3}{\left[ 1+ ( \omega \tau)^2\right]^2}=0
    \label{eq:diff imaginary part}
\end{equation}

\noindent Clearly, the peak is at $\omega=1/\tau$. Besides, it accounts for half of total susceptibility. Fig.\ref{fig:frequency response} is the diagram of the normalized susceptibility against angular frequency.

\subsubsection{Air subtraction}

When measuring the susceptibility, both air and sample contribute to the susceptibility. Thus, we have to remove the susceptibility of the air following Eq.\ref{eq:susceptibility subtraction begin} $\sim$ Eq.\ref{eq:susceptibility subtraction end} or in other word find the relative susceptibility compare to air. However, notice that Eq.\ref{eq:susceptibility subtraction end} is just the real part of the susceptibility but to truly subtract air susceptibility from the real part we want to take ratio between the real part( $\rm{Re}[\chi_{\rm{sample}}] $)and the magnitude of air susceptibility($\chi_{air}$). Further more, we need to be careful of the phase we use here. If we simply use the data int Table \ref{tab:concentration result} we would get a negative ratio, and this lack physics sense because ferrofluid conventionally has a positive susceptibility. Hence, we need to add 180 degree to average phase in order to get the correct results. And you can see our results in Table.\ref{tab:Real-part analysis}


\begin{align}
    \label{eq:susceptibility subtraction begin}
    \bm{\chi}_{\rm{sample}} & =\bm{\chi}_{\rm{mix}}-\bm{\chi}_{\rm{air}} \\
    & = |\chi_{\rm{mix}}| e^{i(\omega t + \theta_{\rm{mix}})}-|\chi_{\rm{air}}| e^{i(\omega t + \theta_{\rm{air}})} \\
    & = \underbrace{\left[ |\chi_{\rm{mix}}| e^{i \theta_{\rm{mix}}} - |\chi_{\rm{air}}| e^{i \theta_{\rm{air}}} \right]}_{\chi_{\rm{sample}}} e^{i \omega t} \\
    \rm{Re}[\chi_{\rm{sample}}] & = |\chi_{\rm{mix}}|\cos{\theta_{\rm{mix}}} -|\chi_{\rm{air}}| \cos{\theta_{\rm{air}}}
    % & = \underbrace{\left[ \chi_{\rm{mix}} \cos{\theta_{\rm{mix}}-\chi_{\rm{air}} \cos{\theta_{\rm{air}}}} \right]}_{F} \cos{(\omega t)} \\ 
    % & - \underbrace{\left[ \chi_{\rm{mix}} \sin{\theta_{\rm{mix}}-\chi_{\rm{air}} \sin{\theta_{\rm{air}}}} \right]}_{G} \sin{(\omega t)} \\
    % & = \sqrt{F^2+G^2} \left[ \frac{F}{\sqrt{F^2+G^2}} \cos{(\omega t)} - \frac{G}{\sqrt{F^2+G^2}} \sin{(\omega t)}\right] \\
    % & = \underbrace{\sqrt{F^2+G^2}}_{\chi_{\rm{sample}}} \cos{(\omega t + \theta_{\rm{sample}})}
    \label{eq:susceptibility subtraction end}
\end{align}

\section{Questions}

\subsection{請比較AC磁化率跟DC磁化率的差別}

AC magnetic susceptibility and DC magnetic susceptibility are two ways to measure how a material responds to an external magnetic field, but they operate under different conditions and reveal different magnetic behaviors:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Frequency of the Magnetic Field:
    
    DC magnetic susceptibility is measured in the presence of a steady, unchanging magnetic field (direct current, or DC field). It reflects the material's magnetic response to a constant magnetic field.
    
    AC magnetic susceptibility is measured when an alternating magnetic field (alternating current, or AC field) is applied. The field changes direction periodically, allowing researchers to study the dynamic response of the material over time.

    \item Measurement of Different Magnetic Properties:
    
    DC susceptibility measures the equilibrium response of a material, showing how much it is magnetized when exposed to a constant magnetic field. It gives insights into the material’s overall magnetic moment and bulk properties.
    
    AC susceptibility gives more information about the dynamic processes in the material, such as the motion of domain walls, relaxation times (Néel or Brownian relaxation in nanoparticles), and other frequency-dependent magnetic behaviors. AC measurements are particularly useful in studying magnetic systems with time-dependent properties.

    \item Applications:
    
    DC measurements are simpler and are often used for basic magnetic characterizations, like identifying whether a material is paramagnetic, ferromagnetic, or diamagnetic.
    
    AC measurements are more sophisticated and are commonly used in research areas that involve magnetic relaxation (e.g., magnetic nanoparticles), studying the frequency response of materials, and detecting magnetic phase transitions.
\end{itemize}

In summary, DC magnetic susceptibility is a static measure of how a material reacts to a constant magnetic field, while AC magnetic susceptibility provides insights into the material's response to a time-varying field, offering more detailed information about dynamic magnetic processes.
